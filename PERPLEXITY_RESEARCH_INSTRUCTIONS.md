### How to Evaluate Sources During Research

**TIER 1 INDICATORS:**
- Published by major national/international outlet
- Has editorial oversight and fact-checking
- Established reputation (decades of operation)

**TIER 3 INDICATORS:**
- Niche publication or blog
- Smaller circulation
- Less established but credible
- Good sourcing and factual
- Examples: Field-specific blogs, fan sites with good reputation

**RED FLAGS (Avoid These):**
- Self-published content (LinkedIn, Medium, personal blogs)
- Press releases from beneficiary's own company
- Paid promotional content
- Social media posts (unless verified official accounts)
- Wikipedia article text (use citations instead)
- Unverified claims
- No editorial oversight

### Key Principles Applied from Comprehensive Methodology

1. **Wikipedia Mining Strategy**
   - Never cite Wikipedia text itself
   - Extract ALL external links and references
   - Follow primary sources from citations

2. **National Team Priority (Athletes)**
   - Satisfies multiple criteria: Awards, Membership, Critical Role, Press
   - Always verify through national federation
   - Include international competition participation

3. **The "5-7 Pages" Rule**
   - Not too few (2-3 = incomplete)
   - Not too many (15-20 = overwhelming/padded)
   - Just right (5-7 = comprehensive but focused)

4. **Source Quality Over Quantity**
   - Better to have 3 Tier 1 sources than 20 Tier 3 sources
   - MMA Junkie (USA Today Sports) > random MMA blog
   - Official rankings (Fight Matrix, Tapology) > unverified claims

5. **Field-Specific Research Nuances**
   - MMA: Rankings + national team focus
   - Tennis: National team selection = game-changer
   - Cricket: Country matters (India/Australia = extremely competitive)
   - Figure Skating: Olympic participation = near-automatic qualifier
