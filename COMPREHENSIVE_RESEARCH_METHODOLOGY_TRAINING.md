## CORE RESEARCH PHILOSOPHY

### The Fundamental Principle

**Initial URLs = Identity Anchors, Not the Complete Picture**

From experienced immigration practice:
> "We don't have enough information to get it through. So I'm asking for more."

When a user provides URLs about a subject, those links serve ONE purpose:
- **WHO are we researching?** (Name, field, basic credentials)

They do NOT represent the full scope of research. The real research begins AFTER we identify the subject.

### The Immigration Paralegal Researcher Mindset

Approach every case as if building an evidentiary record that will be scrutinized by:
- A skeptical immigration officer at USCIS
- An attorney looking for gaps
- A potential RFE (Request for Evidence) reviewer

**Every claim needs:**
- ✅ Third-party validation (not self-promotion)
- ✅ Credible, verifiable sources
- ✅ Quantifiable achievements where possible
- ✅ Comparative context (How do they rank vs. peers?)
- ✅ Multiple corroborating sources (triangulation)

### The Three Research Questions

For every visa petition, answer:

1. **What makes this person extraordinary/distinguished in their field?**
2. **Who ELSE says they're exceptional?** (Third-party validation)
3. **What evidence would convince a skeptical immigration officer who knows nothing about this field?**

## THE RESEARCH PROTOCOL SEQUENCE

### Phase 1: Identity & Field Confirmation (From Initial URLs)

**Objective:** Establish WHO we're researching

**Professional Attorney Method:**
> "First, let's just start by getting him into Google... We're going to start getting all of these profiles."

**Extract from provided URLs:**
- Full legal name and any aliases
- Primary field/industry
- Nationality and country of origin
- Current location/residence
- Basic career timeline
- Major credentials (degrees, certifications)

**Tools:**
- Direct URL review
- Wikipedia page (for identity confirmation only)
- LinkedIn profile
- Official website/bio
- Google search with name

**Output:** A clear identity profile

**Example from Professional Fighter Evaluation:**
```
Name: Alexander Grozin (Grosnick)
Nickname: "The Gangster"
Field: Mixed Martial Arts (MMA)
Record: 25-5
Nationality: Russian
Key Detail: National team experience
```

---

### Phase 2: Deep Discovery (Independent Research)

**Objective:** Find evidence BEYOND the initial URLs

#### **The Wikipedia Mining Strategy**

From professional practice:
> "So in this case, we started looking at the Wikipedia, although we do not use this as a credible source. The thing is that there's a lot of good external links here that we can use."

**How to Mine Wikipedia:**

1. **Find the subject's Wikipedia page** (if exists)
2. **Ignore the summary content** (Wikipedia text itself is not citable)
3. **Scroll to the bottom** → References/Footnotes section
4. **Extract all external links and citations:**
   - Original news articles
   - Academic publications
   - Official award announcements
   - Industry reports
   - Documentary sources
5. **Follow each link** to find primary sources
6. **Use those primary sources** as credible evidence

#### **The Systematic Google Research Method**

From professional live research session:

**Step 1: Google the Name**
```
Search: "Alexander Grozin MMA"
```

**Step 2: Look for Key Profiles**
- Wikipedia
- Tapology
- Sherdog (for MMA)
- BoxRec (for boxing)
- Official sports federation pages
- Major media features

**Step 3: Save Relevant Pages**
> "We're going to start from five to really like five to seven. So let's do five and seven. Page five, seven."

**Why 5-7 pages?** Not too many pages, just the beneficial ones. Save as PDF for evidence package.

**Step 4: Check Rankings**
> "Let's look up Grozin... He's the third best in Russia."

**For MMA specifically:**
- Tapology rankings (regional and international)
- Fight Matrix rankings (world rankings)
- Check where they rank among other fighters

From the research session:
> "His current world ranking is one hundred and nineteen... Grab from one to six pages."

**Why grab multiple pages?**
- Page showing the fighter's individual ranking
- Pages showing them AMONG other world-ranked fighters (context)
- Contact information for the ranking organizations (verification)

**Step 5: Media Coverage**
> "I like to just go like this: [Name] Champion. Fight MMA Junkie is great because it's a heavily publicized outlet with USA Today."

**Why MMA Junkie matters:** It's affiliated with USA Today = major media = Tier 1 source

**Step 6: National Team Research**
> "Let's look for his national team stuff... I'm not finding a lot on this guy."

**When research hits a wall:**
> "We just didn't have enough information to get it through. So I was asking for more."

## SOURCE QUALITY FRAMEWORK

### The Four-Tier System

Based on USCIS credibility assessment and evidentiary weight.

---

### **TIER 1: Gold Standard Sources (9-10/10 Credibility)**

**Characteristics:**
- Major international media with rigorous editorial standards
- Official government or governing body sources
- Peer-reviewed academic publications
- Established institutions with verification processes

**Examples:**
- **Media:** BBC, CNN, The Guardian, New York Times, Wall Street Journal, Reuters, Associated Press
- **Sports:** Official Olympic Committee, FIFA, UEFA, NBA, NFL (official sources), Fight Matrix, Tapology (for combat sports)
- **Entertainment:** Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences (Oscars), Recording Academy (Grammys)
- **Academic:** Nature, Science, peer-reviewed journals with high impact factors
- **Government:** Official award announcements, government recognitions

**From professional practice:**
> "MMA Junkie is great because it's a heavily publicized outlet... with USA Today."

**Why this matters:** Association with major established media elevates credibility

**Research Priority:** HIGH - Always search for Tier 1 sources first

**USCIS Perspective:** These sources are rarely questioned and carry maximum weight

**Perplexity Search Strategy:**
```
"[Subject Name]" site:bbc.com OR site:cnn.com OR site:nytimes.com
"[Subject Name]" [official governing body name]
"[Subject Name]" site:mmajunkie.com OR site:espn.com
```

---

### **TIER 2: Strong Supporting Sources (6-8/10 Credibility)**

**Characteristics:**
- Established regional media
- Industry-specific authoritative publications
- Professional association publications
- Verified institutional sources
- Respected trade publications

**Examples:**
- **Media:** Evening Standard (UK), Metro (UK), regional newspapers with broad reach
- **Combat Sports:** Sherdog, MMA Fighting, Boxing Scene, The Ring Magazine
- **General Sports:** Sports Illustrated, BBC Sport, regional sports federations
- **Business:** Forbes, Fortune, Bloomberg, Business Insider (non-listicle articles)
- **Academic:** University press releases, department newsletters, conference proceedings
- **Tech:** TechCrunch, Wired, MIT Technology Review

**From professional Research:**
- Tapology rankings (MMA fighters)
- Fight Matrix (world rankings)
- CricInfo (cricket statistics)
- IMDB (entertainment credits)

**Research Priority:** MEDIUM-HIGH - Excellent supporting evidence

**USCIS Perspective:** Generally accepted but may require corroboration for controversial claims

**Perplexity Search Strategy:**
```
"[Subject Name]" site:sherdog.com OR site:tapology.com OR site:fightmatrix.com
"[Subject Name]" [industry publication name]
"[Subject Name]" professional recognition OR industry recognition
```

---

### **TIER 3: Supplementary Sources (4-6/10 Credibility)**

**Characteristics:**
- Biographical aggregators with some editorial oversight
- Local/regional media with limited reach
- Fan sites with editorial content (not user-generated)
- Personal websites with professional information
- Podcast appearances
- Blog features from established platforms

**Examples:**
- Wikipedia (for information gathering, NOT as evidence)
- Famous Birthdays (biographical data)
- IMDb (basic information, not reviews)
- Local newspapers
- Regional magazines
- Established blogs with clear authorship

**Research Priority:** LOW - Use only as supporting evidence, never as primary

**USCIS Perspective:** Shows popularity but not necessarily distinction; needs corroboration

**From professional practice:**
> "We started looking at the Wikipedia, although we do not use this as a credible source. The thing is that there's a lot of good external links here that we can use."

**How to Use Tier 3 Sources:**
1. **Mine them for Tier 1-2 source leads**
2. **Use for biographical timeline confirmation**
3. **Never cite them as primary evidence**
4. **Extract referenced sources from footnotes**

---

### **TIER 4: Weak/Non-Credible Sources (1-3/10 Credibility)**

**Characteristics:**
- Social media posts
- Self-published materials
- Unverified websites
- PR releases without media pickup
- User-generated content platforms

**Examples:**
- Instagram/TikTok/Twitter posts
- Personal blog posts
- Press releases not picked up by media
- Wikipedia article text (as evidence)
- Fandom wikis
- Unverified websites

**Research Priority:** AVOID - Do not use as evidence

**USCIS Perspective:** Not acceptable as evidence of distinction

**Exception:** Social media can be used for:
- Verification of basic facts (dates, locations)
- Showing engagement metrics (follower counts) as SUPPLEMENTARY to other evidence
- **Never as primary evidence of achievement**

## VISA-TYPE SPECIFIC RESEARCH STRATEGIES

### EB-1A: Extraordinary Ability (Permanent Residence)

**Standard:** Top of the field

**Research Focus:** 3 of 10 criteria required

From professional philosophy:
> "With EB-1, you really need to have some more direct proof that you are one of the top in your field."

#### **When EB-1A Works:**

**Fencer Example:**
> "Olympic participation, bronze medal in World Cup, first olympian from their country... this is a excellent candidate."

**What Makes It Strong:**
- Olympic medal (any color)
- World championship medals
- "First from country" achievements
- National team at highest level

#### **When EB-2 NIW May Be Better:**

From data scientist evaluation:
> "They have a good profile for sure... but the thing is with EB-1, you really need to have some more direct proof... But there's another way that makes a lot of sense for data scientists right now."

**EB-2 NIW Strategy:**
- Connect to national priorities (AI Task Force, Biden administration initiatives)
- Work addresses shortage in US
- National importance of field
- Don't need to prove "top of field," just that work matters

**From professional practice:**
> "The EB-II national interest waiver is a great alternative for a self-sponsored visa that's apart from the employer."

**Timing consideration:**
> "I behoove you to try the national interest waiver definitely before there is a change in administration."

---

### O-1A: Extraordinary Ability (Non-Immigrant)

**Standard:** Sustained national or international acclaim

**Research Focus:** 3 of 8 criteria (similar to EB-1A but slightly lower bar)

#### **O-1A vs P-1 Decision Matrix**

From esports case:
> "I would support an O-1 visa over a P-1 is just because as leagues stop and fold, USCIS is getting more aggressive with revoking P-1s."

**O-1A Advantages:**
- Not tied to specific competitions
- Can do content creation, coaching, seminars
- Can start businesses
- More flexibility if injured
- Broader work authorization

**Research Strategy for O-1A:**
- Olympic participation
- National team experience (senior or youth)
- World rankings
- Major tournament results
- Media coverage of achievements

**From track athlete case:**
> "This athlete does have Olympic participation, there's the opportunity to do the O-1 visa... it's not limited to competition."

**Activities allowed on O-1 but not P-1:**
- Making content
- Starting businesses
- Coaching
- Seminars
- Multiple revenue streams

---

### P-1A: Internationally Recognized Athlete

**Standard:** Must compete with internationally recognized team or competition

**Research Priority #1: National Team**

From multiple the attorney transcripts:

**Soccer player:**
> "Usually when we have a good track record of national team ability in sports, that's a great, great, pretty much certain case for an O-A visa."

**Cricket player:**
> "The national team caps and how successful those national teams were."

**What to Research:**
1. **National team confirmation** (most important)
2. **Number of caps/appearances**
3. **Level:** Senior (strongest) vs Youth (strong) vs Lower tier (weaker)
4. **Major tournaments** (World Cup, Olympics, Continental Championship)
5. **Results/achievements with national team**

**From Ghana golfer case:**

Even without high international rankings, can qualify with:
1. **Strong in-country expert endorsements**
2. **Association with national governing body**
3. **Regional tournament success**
4. **Cross-border media coverage**

> "If you have won national tournaments in your country and the coverage of that, when it does spread to experts that are outside of country or even media publishing that's outside of your country, that's enough."

---

### O-1B: Arts/Entertainment

**Different from O-1A**

From figure skater case:
> "We've got to choose between doing the 01A or the 01B... sometimes we give USCIS the option of like choosing which one fits the category."

**When to use O-1B:**
- Influencing/content creation primary
- Post-athletic career entertainment
- Acting/performing focus
- Media/social media influence

**Research Focus:**
- Social media following (SUPPLEMENTARY only)
- IMDB credits
- Entertainment industry awards
- Brand partnerships
- Content creation portfolio

**From professional practice:**
> "If it's that your influencing is more compelling than your athletics... then it's the O1A that will carry the day."

## PERPLEXITY API INTEGRATION & QUERY TEMPLATES

### The Perplexity Research Workflow

Based on professional systematic approach translated to AI-assisted research.

---

### **Phase 1: Identity Confirmation (2-3 queries)**

```
Query 1: "Who is [Full Name]? Provide comprehensive background including field, nationality, major achievements, current activities, and career timeline."

Query 2: "[Full Name] [Field] biography OR career history OR professional background"

Query 3 (Sport-Specific): "[Full Name] [Sport] rankings OR record OR statistics"
```

**Expected Output:**
- Full name and aliases
- Field/industry
- Nationality and location
- Career highlights
- Current status/record

---

### **Phase 2: Criterion-Specific Deep Dives**

#### **For Combat Sports Athletes:**

**Rankings Research:**
```
Query: "[Name] world ranking [sport]"
Query: "[Name] Tapology ranking" (MMA)
Query: "[Name] BoxRec ranking" (Boxing)
Query: "[Name] Fight Matrix ranking" (MMA)
```

**National Team Research:**
```
Query: "[Name] national team [country] [sport]"
Query: "[Country] national [sport] team roster"
Query: "[Name] represented [country] international competition"
```

**Media Coverage:**
```
Query: "[Name] site:mmajunkie.com OR site:sherdog.com OR site:espn.com"
Query: "[Name] fight OR bout OR match feature OR profile"
```

**Championship/Tournament History:**
```
Query: "[Name] championship OR title fight OR tournament"
Query: "[Name] [major tournament name]" (e.g., "UFC" "Bellator" "ONE Championship")
```

---

#### **For Olympic Athletes:**

**Olympic Verification:**
```
Query: "[Name] Olympics [year] OR Olympic athlete [country]"
Query: "[Name] site:olympics.com OR site:olympic.org"
Query: "[Name] Olympic results OR Olympic performance"
```

**From professional fencer case:**
> "They are the first fencer from their country that was able to compete in the Olympics"

## RED FLAGS & COMMON PITFALLS

### Case Quality Red Flags

From professional evaluation experience:

---

### **Red Flag #1: Insufficient Information Despite Search**

**Warning Sign:**
> "I'm not finding a lot on this guy... We need more info."

**What This Indicates:**
- May not be prominent enough yet
- Or information exists but not publicly available
- Or searching wrong places/terms

**Action:**
- Request specific documentation from client
- Provide guidance on what's needed
- May need to wait to build stronger profile

---

### **Red Flag #2: College-Only Achievements**

From tennis player case:
- College: Division I ✓
- Conference awards: Player of Week (not Year)
- Professional ranking: #700 (not strong)
- No international team experience

**Assessment:**
> "It'd be a stretch to do the O-1."

**What This Means:**
- Strong for P-1 (if has competition schedule)
- Not yet ready for O-1
- May qualify for O-1 later with:
  - Higher professional ranking
  - National team experience
  - Major tournament wins
  - Coaching track record

---

### **Red Flag #3: Self-Reported Achievements Without Verification**

**From professional systematic approach:**

Always verify through multiple sources:
- Wikipedia mention → Find external links
- Ranking claim → Check official ranking site
- National team claim → Verify with federation
- Media coverage claim → Find actual articles

**Never rely on:**
- Client's resume alone
- Self-published materials
- Social media posts
- Unverified websites

---

### **Red Flag #4: Misunderstanding "Internationally Recognized"**

**Common Error:** Assuming any international activity = internationally recognized

**Reality Check:**

**P-1 Requires:**
- Competition at events that require internationally recognized athletes
- Not just any international competition

**Example Distinctions:**

**✅ Internationally Recognized:**
- Olympics
- World Championships
- Major professional leagues (UFC, Bellator, ATP, WTA)
- Continental championships

**❌ Not Internationally Recognized:**
- Small regional tournaments abroad
- Exhibition matches
- Minor league international tours
- Amateur competitions without selection process

---

### Research Process Pitfalls

#### **Pitfall #1: Stopping Research Too Soon**

**The Problem:**
From professional method, he doesn't stop at first Google result.

**Systematic Approach:**
1. Google search (get overview)
2. Multiple ranking systems (Tapology + Fight Matrix + Sherdog)
3. Media coverage (MMA Junkie, ESPN, etc.)
4. National team verification (federation websites)
5. Tournament history
6. Contact information for organizations

**Don't stop at Wikipedia or first result.**

---

#### **Pitfall #2: Not Capturing Context**

**From professional practice:**
> "Show them among other world-ranked fighters."

**Why This Matters:**
- Not just "I'm ranked #119"
- But "#119 among world's best fighters" (visual context)
- Shows COMPANY they're in
- Provides comparative framework

**What to Capture:**
- Individual achievement page
- List/table showing them among peers
- Organization contact information
- Methodology/criteria for ranking

---

#### **Pitfall #3: Saving Too Many or Too Few Pages**

**From professional practice:**
> "We're going to start from five to really like five to seven."

**Why 5-7 Pages:**
- Too few (1-2): Looks incomplete, lacks context
- Too many (15-20): Overwhelming, appears padded
- Just right (5-7): Comprehensive but focused

**What to Save:**
- Main profile page
- Rankings page
- Key achievements page
- Media coverage (select best)
- Organization "About Us"
- Contact verification

---

#### **Pitfall #4: Ignoring Tier Quality**

**Not all sources equal.**

From Tallulah Metcalfe analysis:
> "Better to have 3 Tier 1 sources than 20 Tier 3 sources."

**Quality > Quantity**

**Bad Approach:**
- Gather 30 local newspaper clippings
- Multiple fan site mentions
- Social media posts
- Self-published materials

**Good Approach:**
- 3 major media features (BBC, ESPN, USA Today)
- 2 official ranking systems
- 1 national federation confirmation
- 5-7 pages of solid documentation

---

#### **Pitfall #5: Not Documenting "What's Missing"**

**From professional method:**

Always document:
- What was found
- What's still needed
- Specific requests for client
- Where to look next

**Create:**
- Evidence matrix with gaps marked
- Specific information request list
- Guidance on where client can find info

> "The credits here aren't as strong as what your athletics are showing."

**Step 3: Social Media Review**
> "We see you have a pretty good following on Instagram."

**But...**
> "Of all of this, I would say that the Olympic skating, national team and the appearances that you did with the theatrical skating carry the day."

**Decision:**
> "They carry the day under 01A as a world-class skater."

**Why O-1A (Not O-1B):**
1. Olympic participation strongest evidence
2. Can include ALL activities under O-1A
3. Disney on Ice = critical capacity evidence
4. Influencing as supplementary
5. More flexibility

**Structure:**
> "We'll use that so we can show both the athletics that you did in the competitive side, the theatrical side and also use these placements to benefit too."

**Lessons:**
1. Lead with strongest evidence (Olympics)
2. Can combine multiple activities under O-1A
3. Social media = supporting, not leading
4. O-1A offers more flexibility than O-1B

### **Case Study 5: Data Scientist (EB-1A vs EB-2 NIW)**

**Profile:**
- Good career in data science
- Worked for prestigious companies
- No major publications or awards
- Wants self-sponsored permanent residence

**Initial Assessment:**
> "They have a good profile for sure... but the thing is with EB-1, you really need to have some more direct proof that you are one of the top in your field."

**EB-1A Challenges:**
- No publications about projects
- No special group memberships
- Not clearly "top of field"

**Alternative Path: EB-2 NIW**

**Why NIW Makes Sense:**

**1. Government Priorities:**
> "The Biden administration has an artificial intelligence task force that's not just for AI, but it also includes data science."

**2. National Interest Argument:**
> "There's an immigrant pathway that's self-sponsored called the National Interest Waiver... for foreign nationals that are in the interest of the United States to grant permanent residence despite not having a job offer."

**3. Field Shortage:**
- US needs data scientists
- Government actively recruiting
- Skills in national interest

**Recommendation:**
> "The EB-II national interest waiver is a great alternative for a self-sponsored visa that's apart from the employer."

**Timing Consideration:**
> "I behoove you to try the national interest waiver definitely before there is a change in administration... to be safe, I would get it done soon."

**Lessons:**
1. EB-1A vs EB-2 NIW serve different candidates
2. Government priorities matter for NIW
3. Timing considerations important
4. Field shortage strengthens NIW case

### **The Essential Research Principles**

**1. Initial URLs are starting points, not endpoints**

From professional practice:
> "We started looking at the Wikipedia... The thing is that there's a lot of good external links here that we can use."

**2. Third-party validation is everything**

Not self-promotion. Not social media. Official rankings, national team verification, major media coverage.

**3. Quality over quantity in evidence**

From source analysis:
> "Better to have 3 Tier 1 sources than 20 Tier 3 sources."

From professional practice:
> "We're going to start from five to really like five to seven pages" (strategic selection)

**4. Tier 1-2 sources carry the case**

MMA Junkie (USA Today), Fight Matrix, Tapology, ESPN, national federations.

**5. Every field has its own way of proving excellence**

- Combat sports: Rankings + national team
- Tennis: ATP/WTA rankings + tournaments
- Cricket: National team from powerhouse country
- Figure skating: Olympics + Disney on Ice

**6. National team experience is gold for athletes**

From multiple transcripts:
> "Usually when we have a good track record of national team ability in sports, that's a great, great, pretty much certain case for an O-A visa."

**7. "First from country" adds major value**

From fencer case:
> "They are the first fencer from their country that was able to compete in the Olympics, which is great for original contributions."

**8. Research like building an evidentiary record**

Systematic, methodical, verifiable, multiple sources.

**9. Know when to pivot to different visa**

From tennis player:
> "I think in this case, it'd be a stretch to do the O-1. She'd be much better off with the P-1."

**10. Honest assessment serves client better than false optimism**

From MMA fighter:
> "We just didn't have enough information to get it through. So I was asking for more."
