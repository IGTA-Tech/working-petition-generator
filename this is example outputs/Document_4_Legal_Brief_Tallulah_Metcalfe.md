## III. LEGAL STANDARDS FOR O-1B CLASSIFICATION

### A. Statutory Requirements

The O-1 nonimmigrant classification was established by the Immigration Act of 1990 and is codified at Section 101(a)(15)(O) of the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), 8 U.S.C. § 1101(a)(15)(O). The statute provides for temporary admission of aliens "who have extraordinary ability in the sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics" or "extraordinary achievement in motion picture or television production."

The O-1 category is subdivided into O-1A and O-1B classifications:

- **O-1A:** For individuals with extraordinary ability in sciences, education, business, or athletics
- **O-1B:** For individuals with extraordinary ability in the arts or extraordinary achievement in motion picture or television production

This petition seeks O-1B classification based on Ms. Metcalfe's extraordinary ability in the arts, specifically in the field of digital content creation and social media entertainment.

The statutory standard for O-1B classification requires that the beneficiary demonstrate "extraordinary ability" in the arts, defined as "distinction." INA § 101(a)(46). The statute further defines "distinction" as "a high level of achievement in the field of arts evidenced by a degree of skill and recognition substantially above that ordinarily encountered to the extent that a person described as prominent is renowned, leading, or well-known in the field of arts."

Critically, the O-1B standard of "distinction" is explicitly lower than the "extraordinary ability" standard applied to O-1A petitions. This distinction reflects Congressional recognition that artistic fields often involve more subjective evaluation criteria than scientific or athletic fields, and that artistic achievement may be demonstrated through different forms of evidence than those applicable to other fields.

### B. Regulatory Criteria

The regulatory requirements for O-1B classification are set forth at 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv). The regulations provide two alternative paths to establishing eligibility:

**Path 1: Major International Award**

Evidence that the beneficiary has received, or been nominated for, significant national or international awards or prizes in the particular field, such as an Academy Award, Emmy, Grammy, or Director's Guild Award. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(A).

**Path 2: At Least Three of Six Regulatory Criteria**

If the beneficiary has not received a major international award, the petition must include evidence of at least three of the following:

1. **Performed or will perform services as a lead or starring participant in productions or events which have a distinguished reputation** as evidenced by critical reviews, advertisements, publicity releases, publications, contracts, or endorsements. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(B)(1).

2. **Achieved national or international recognition** for achievements, as shown by critical reviews or other published materials by or about the beneficiary in major newspapers, trade journals, magazines, or other publications. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(B)(2).

3. **Performed or will perform in a lead, starring, or critical role for organizations and establishments that have a distinguished reputation** as evidenced by articles in newspapers, trade journals, publications, or testimonials. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(B)(3).

4. **A record of major commercial or critically acclaimed successes**, as shown by such indicators as title, rating, or standing in the field, box office receipts, motion picture or television ratings, and other occupational achievements reported in trade journals, major newspapers, or other publications. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(B)(4).

5. **Received significant recognition for achievements from organizations, critics, government agencies, or other recognized experts** in the field in which the beneficiary is engaged, with the testimonials clearly indicating the author's authority, expertise, and knowledge of the beneficiary's achievements. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(B)(5).

6. **A high salary or other substantial remuneration for services in relation to others in the field**, as shown by contracts or other reliable evidence. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(B)(6).

The regulations further provide that if the above criteria do not readily apply to the beneficiary's occupation, the petitioner may submit comparable evidence to establish eligibility. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(iv)(C).

### C. Standard of Proof

The applicable standard of proof for O-1B petitions is "preponderance of the evidence." Matter of Chawathe, 25 I&N Dec. 369, 375-76 (AAO 2010). Under this standard, the petitioner must establish that the claimed facts are "more likely than not" or "probably" true. The preponderance of the evidence standard requires that the evidence demonstrate that the applicant's claim is "probably true," where the determination of "truth" is made based on the factual circumstances of each individual case. Matter of E-M-, 20 I&N Dec. 77, 79-80 (Comm. 1989).

This standard is less demanding than the "clear and convincing evidence" standard or the "beyond a reasonable doubt" standard applied in other legal contexts. The petitioner need not eliminate all doubt regarding the beneficiary's qualifications, but rather must present sufficient evidence that, when weighed in totality, makes it more likely than not that the beneficiary meets the regulatory criteria.

Importantly, USCIS must evaluate the evidence in totality, considering both the quantity and quality of evidence presented. Kazarian v. USCIS, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010) (establishing two-step analysis for extraordinary ability petitions). While Kazarian specifically addressed EB-1A petitions, its analytical framework has been applied by analogy to O-1 petitions, requiring adjudicators to first determine whether the petitioner has submitted qualifying evidence for each criterion claimed, and then evaluate the evidence in totality to determine whether the beneficiary has demonstrated the required level of achievement.

### D. Applicable USCIS Policy Guidance

USCIS has issued several policy memoranda and guidance documents that inform the adjudication of O-1B petitions:

**USCIS Policy Manual, Volume 2, Part M**

The USCIS Policy Manual provides comprehensive guidance on O-1 classifications. Relevant provisions include:

- Recognition that the "arts" includes "any field of creative activity or endeavor such as, but not limited to, fine arts, visual arts, culinary arts, and performing arts." USCIS Policy Manual, Vol. 2, Part M, Ch. 4(A).

- Clarification that the O-1B standard of "distinction" means "a high level of achievement in the field of arts evidenced by a degree of skill and recognition substantially above that ordinarily encountered to the extent that a person described as prominent is renowned, leading, or well-known in the field of arts." Id.

- Guidance that adjudicators should consider the totality of evidence and not apply overly rigid standards that fail to account for the unique nature of different artistic fields. Id.

**2010 Neufeld Memo on Comparable Evidence**

In a December 22, 2010 memorandum, USCIS clarified the use of comparable evidence when standard regulatory criteria do not readily apply to a beneficiary's occupation. The memo emphasizes that adjudicators should exercise flexibility in evaluating evidence and should not reject comparable evidence merely because it differs from the examples listed in the regulations. This guidance is particularly relevant to emerging fields like digital content creation, where traditional evidence types may not fully capture the nature of achievement in the field.

**2018 Policy Memo on Deference**

USCIS guidance on deference to prior approvals, while subsequently modified, established principles regarding consistency in adjudication and the importance of considering all evidence presented. These principles remain relevant to O-1B adjudications.

**Adjudicator's Field Manual**

The Adjudicator's Field Manual provides additional guidance on evaluating evidence quality, assessing credibility of sources, and applying regulatory criteria to diverse factual scenarios. Chapter 33.3 specifically addresses O classifications and emphasizes that adjudicators should consider the specific nature of the beneficiary's field when evaluating evidence.

**Recognition of Digital Media and Social Media as Legitimate Arts Fields**

While not addressed in specific policy memoranda, USCIS has increasingly recognized digital content creation, social media entertainment, and related fields as legitimate areas of artistic endeavor eligible for O-1B classification. Numerous O-1B petitions have been approved for social media influencers, YouTube creators, TikTok personalities, and digital artists, establishing precedent that these fields constitute "arts" within the meaning of the regulations.

The artistic nature of digital content creation is evidenced by:

- **Creative Expression:** Digital content requires artistic decisions regarding visual composition, narrative structure, aesthetic presentation, and audience engagement

- **Technical Skill:** Professional-quality digital content requires mastery of filming, editing, lighting, sound design, and platform-specific technical requirements

- **Cultural Impact:** Digital content creators shape cultural trends, influence consumer behavior, and contribute to contemporary artistic movements

- **Commercial Recognition:** Major entertainment companies, brands, and media organizations invest billions of dollars in digital content and influencer partnerships, recognizing these creators as legitimate entertainment professionals

- **Industry Infrastructure:** The digital content creation field has developed professional infrastructure including talent agencies, management companies, industry publications, awards programs, and professional organizations—all hallmarks of established artistic fields

**Burden of Proof**

The petitioner bears the burden of establishing eligibility for the requested classification by a preponderance of the evidence. 8 C.F.R. § 214.2(o)(3)(ii). However, once the petitioner has submitted evidence that facially meets the regulatory criteria, the burden shifts to USCIS to articulate specific, substantive reasons for denial. Matter of Chawathe, 25 I&N Dec. at 376.

USCIS may not deny a petition based on speculation, unsupported assumptions, or application of standards not found in the regulations. Adjudicators must evaluate the evidence actually presented and may not impose requirements beyond those specified in the regulations. Kazarian, 596 F.3d at 1121-22.
